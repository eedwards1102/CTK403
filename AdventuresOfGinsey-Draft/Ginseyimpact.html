Ginsey's Impact

About Ginsey

Ginsey is my guide dog for the blind. On the job, Ginsey guides me by relaying feedback through a harness. I give Ginsey directions on where to walk, and he leads the way forward. Ginsey relays stops, necessary speed changes, and the direction of travel by harness feedback.

Guide dogs look out for obstacles in the walking path, dropoffs like stairs, walking around pedestrians and still crowds, ensure their handler walks in a straight line, and much more. It is important to note that guide dogs are not GPSs, and it is still up to the handler to decide where to go. However, dogs can learn routes over time. For example, by walking a route routinely, or with specific training, guide dogs can remember the route to your morning coffee shop. They can even remember specific destinations, like the door to your classroom in a big hallway, or even your usual assigned seat. Guide dogs also learn object-specific commands. For example, I can request Ginsey to find curbs, elevators, doors, and chairs.

This brings me to another point. Guide dogs are trained on another skill called intelligent disobedience. While it is on me to gauge my surroundings, and know when to cross the street, Ginsey still looks out to make sure the commands I'm giving him are safe. If I'm standing atop stairs or a big dropoff and don't know it, and tell him to go forward, Ginsey will refuse the command. If I try to step out into a street with a car coming, Ginsey will refuse the command and back me away from the street. Having this extra lookout when I travel makes me feel so much safer.

How does Ginsey help me?

Working with Ginsey has absolutely changed my life in so many ways. You learn so much about companionship, trust, and confidence.

Going everywhere with a dog, they become your number 1 sidekick. You learn so much about how they communicate, and the dog learns how to work with you. When people ask how long you train with your dog, I tell them formal training may only be two weeks, but the most training happens over the next year when you are home. That's when you learn how to work with your specific dog.

Working with Ginsey, I have also learned so much about trust. When you are working with a guide dog, you have to trust them 100 percent. I think many of us default to looking out for our own safety when we travel, but that totally changes once you have a guide dog. If Ginsey stops in the middle of a sidewalk, and I don't think we should stop, the head turns, and I know I'm getting the side eye. Spoiler, 9 times out of 10, he made the right choice. The handler and the dog, me and Ginsey, really become a team, always looking out for each other.

For me, Ginsey has given me the independence and confidence I always wanted. Canes and dogs are both great, everyone's needs are different. But for me, Ginsey was the right call. When I'm walking, it feels so freeing to walk at mock speed, weave around pedestrians in a crowd like they're not even there, and know that Ginsey is looking out for scary obstacles like stairs or a big crack in the sidewalk.

Having Ginsey has also been great socially. Sometimes you get too many eyes on you in public, but for the most part it's been great. Ginsey gives people a reason to approach and chat with me, and I have been able to meet some great friends this way.

Having Ginsey has been like a set of eyes for me. He's looking out for me, I travel with much more confidence, and it is all such an empowering feeling. Ginsey is also a best friend. Working with a dog 24/7, you build such a deep bond. Being a team with Ginsey has made me a much happier and confident person.
